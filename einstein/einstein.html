Introduction

Albert Einstein is well known as one of the smartest people to ever live. This is partially due to his contributions to special and general relativity, two fields of physics that are known to be very confusing and mind-boggling for most. To add to this feat, he managed to come up with his theories without any heavy duty lab equipment or a complex mathematical background. He did it all just by thinking really hard. Fortunately, you can learn an important part of Einstein's confusing concepts without all of the intense thinking, right here! We will teach you how to derive the Lorentz Transformation, an algebraic equation that allows you to predict time and space dilation based on the relative speeds of objects.

The Principle of Relativity

The Principle of Relativity is a crucial law of relativistic physics, as well as all other branches of physics. Say we have two people in space, Einstein and his evil twin, Alberto, one moving to the right and the other moving to the left.

                   

For Einstein, it seems that he is standing still, while Alberto is moving to the left.

                                               

For Alberto, it seems as if he is standing still while Einstein is moving to the right.

                                         

The principle of relativity states that motion is relative. While we may seem to be standing still on Earth, we are still moving with respect to the sun, or the international space station, or another planet 500 light years away.This means that the motion of one thing depends on that of another, and objects that move may be at rest with respect to others and resting objects may be moving with respect to others. There is no way to be at absolute rest or in absolute motion. No matter what, you will be moving relative to something. In addition to this, the principle of relativity states that no matter how fast you may be moving relative to someone or something else, the laws of physics still apply in the exact same way. This means that there is no experiment that can be done to determine whether you are moving or in a state of "absolute rest" (which is impossible).

Constant Speed of Light

Although it souds strange and counter-intuitive, the speed of light holds the same value, 299,792,458 m/s (186,000 mph), relative to everything! For example, if you were standing still and turned on a flashlight, the light from the flashlight would move at 299,792,458 m/s. If you were driving a car at 25 m/s (56 mph) and turned on the headlights, the light would still move at 299,792,458 m/s. Even if the car was moving at 100,000,000 m/s, the light from its headlights would still move at the speed of light, 299,792,458 m/s. This is a crucial concept in relativity and is the basis of the concepts discussed later.

Invariance of the Interval

Imagine that Einstein is in a laboratory on the surface of the Earth and Alberto is on a moving rocket, and each one has a laser pointer. If each one pointed his laser straight up at a mirror 1 meter above so that it would bounce back down, the path that Einstein's light travelled would be a straight line, straight up, then off of the mirror and straight back down.

Einstein's Light

If Alberto shines his laser pointer at the mirror, the path of the light will be triangular because Alberto is moving sideways (relative to Einstein). The path of the light in this case will look like this:

Alberto's Light

Alberto's light path forms a triangle, which is very useful in deriving the Lorentz Transformation. The longest side of the right triangle, also known as the hypotenuse, is the direct path that the light travels. In the straight line path, this length is denoted t', while in the triangle it is t. The base of the line is denoted x', or x in the triangle, while the height in both cases is 1 meter. By using the Pythagorean Theorem (Proven Here ) we know that 12 + x2 = t2 and 12 + x'2 = t'2. If we subtract 1 from each side, we get x2 = t2 -1 and x'2 = t'2 -1. Then, by subtracting t2 and t'2, we get x2 - t2 = -1 and x'2 - t'2= -1. Because both equations are equal to -1, they must be equal to each other, so we get x2 - t2 = x'2 - t'2. Because x'=0 (The base of a line is zero) t'2 = x2 - t2.

Derivation of the Lorentz Transformation

Part 1

First, we are going derive the first two coefficients of the Lorentz Transformation. The entire Lorentz Transformation will be in the form of x = Ax' + Bt' and t = Bx' + At', but to simplify things we are going to let (t',x') be the coordinates in the rocket frame. In the rocket frame x' always equals zero. Also, for this derivation we are going to use Βr to represent the speed one reference frame moves with respect to the other. For calculations use Βr = v/c

(1) From the standard physics equation speed = distance over time:

$$\frac{x}{t}= \beta_r$$

(2) Multiply both sides by t.

$$x = \beta_rt$$

(3) Square both sides

$$x^{2} = { \beta_r}^{2}t^{2}$$

(4) From the Invariance of the Interval, we have:

$$t^{2} - x^{2} = t'^{2} - x'^{2} = t'^{2}$$

(5) Substitute equation (3) for x2

$$t^{2} - { \beta_r}^{2}t^{2} = t'^{2}$$

(6) Factor out t2

$$t^{2}(1-{ \beta_r}^{2}) = t'^2$$

(7) Divide both sides to isolate t2

$$t^{2}= \frac{t'^2}{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}$$

(8) Take the square root of both sides to solve for t.

$$t= \frac{t'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}$$

(9) Solve for x. Remember from equation (2) that x = Βr t

$$x = \frac{\beta_rt'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}$$

Part 2

So now we have succesfully derived the Lorentz Transformation to go from the rocket frame to the laboratory frame. But wait, you might object, what if x' was nonzero? Doesn't x depend on both t' and x'? Well, you would be right! For the second part of the Lorentz Transformation, we write that t = t'(1 - Βr2)(-1/2) + Ax' and x = Βrt'(1 - Βr2)(-1/2). Now we just solve for A and B and we are all done! :)

(1) From Part 1 and knowing that x and t depend on both x' and t':

$$t= \frac{t'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}} + Ax'\\ x = \frac{\beta_rt'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}+Bx'$$

(2) From the Invariance of the Interval:

$$t^{2} - x^{2} = t'^{2} - x'^{2}$$

(3) Substitute t and x from equation (1)

$$(\frac{t'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}} + Ax')^{2} - (\frac{\beta_rt'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}+Bx')^{2} = t'^{2} - x'^{2}$$

(4) Expand the two squared terms. Remember that (a + b)2 = a2 + 2ab + b2

$$(\frac{t'^2}{1- \beta_r^{2}} + \frac{2Ax't'}{ \sqrt{1- \beta_r^{2}}} +A^2x'^2) - (\frac{\beta_r^2t'^2}{1- \beta_r^{2}} + \frac{2\beta_rBx't'}{ \sqrt{1- \beta_r^{2}}} +B^2x'^2) = t'^2 - x'^2$$

(5) Combine like terms

$$t'^2 + \frac{2(A-\beta_rB)}{ \sqrt{1-\beta_r^2}}x't' + (A^2 - B^2)x'^2 = t'^2 - x'^2$$

(6) Almost there! For the left side to simplify to equal the right, 2 things must happen. 1. x't' must disappear so the second coefficient must equal 0.

$$A - \beta_rB=0\\A=\beta_rB$$

(7) And 2: The third coefficient must equal -1 to get (-1)x2 on the right.

$$A^2-B^2 = -1\\B^2-A^2=1$$

(8) Now we solve the system of equations for A and B using equations (6) and (7). First subsitute Βr B for A.

$$B^2-(\beta_rB)^2=1$$

9) Factor out B2

$$B^2(1 -\beta_r^2) =1$$

(10) Divide through to isolate B2 and take the square root to solve for B.

$$B= \frac{1}{ \sqrt{(1 -\beta_r^2)}}$$

(11) Use equation (6) to solve for A.

$$A= \frac{\beta_r}{ \sqrt{(1 -\beta_r^2)}}$$

(12) That's It! The Lorentz Transformation:

$$t= \frac{t'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}} + \frac{\beta_rx'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}\\ x = \frac{\beta_rt'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}+ \frac{x'}{ \sqrt{(1-{ \beta_r}^{2})}}$$

Conclusion

Now Albert and Alberto Einstein can communicate and talk about the same events no matter how fast Alberto is moving in his rocket ship! The Lorentz Transformation allows us to understand how spacetime changes based on relative velocities. We can convert (x, t) coordinates from one reference frame to any other!

Another fascinating result of the Lorentz Transformation is that it gives us the universal speed limit. If we let Βr go to 1, or equivalently let v go to c
(remember Βr = v/c), the denominator of the Lorentz equations goes to 0, so the (x,t) coordinates will go to infinity! This means that c is the maximum possible speed for anything. If v > c, we are forced to take the square root of a negative number, which, of course, is impossible.

Also to note are the ideas of time dilation and length contraction. Simply put, as you move at faster speeds, your clocks seem to be slower and your meter sticks actually get shorter. See if you can prove this by using the Lorentz Transformation on your own!
